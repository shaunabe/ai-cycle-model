How the levers connect

What's different from prior cycles

The revenue is real — and still an order of magnitude short of the capex

Dot-com bears were right that 1999 revenue was mostly fictional. This time enterprise AI spend is real and compounding fast — but it remains a small fraction of the roughly half-trillion dollars of annual infrastructure capex. The bull case requires elasticity above 1: cheaper tokens must expand usage faster than they deflate price. That's the single most important slider above.

Deflation is faster than bandwidth ever was

Bandwidth got cheap over a decade; frontier-equivalent tokens got ~80% cheaper in a year, and open-weight models put a hard floor under what anyone can charge for mid-tier intelligence. In 2000, the commodity (fiber) was the overbuilt asset. Here the commodity may be the model itself — value migrates to applications, distribution, and proprietary data, which is where seed-stage exposure lives.

Physical constraints are both the brake and the moat

The fiber glut had no power bill. Today, grid interconnects, advanced packaging, and even helium supply ration how fast capacity can grow. Constraints cap the revenue line — but they also prevent the classic overbuild that vaporized telecom pricing, and they hand a scarcity premium to whoever already holds energized capacity. That's why the infrastructure slider pushes the multiple up even as it pushes growth down.

Incumbents have the budgets; startups have the throughput

Survey adoption is near-universal, but realized productivity inside large organizations is throttled by workflow, compliance, and legacy stacks — the pilots-to-P&L conversion rate. AI-native startups skip that overhead entirely. If realization stays low while startup-led value share rises, incumbent software multiples compress even as aggregate AI usage grows. A boom for builders can coexist with a bust for index holders.

The bubble, if it is one, is disproportionately private

In 2000 the excess was listed and repriced in weeks. Today the largest AI valuations sit in private hands, marked quarterly, with downside stickiness — which is exactly what the green line models. The gap between the blue and green lines at month 24 is the unrealized repricing sitting in LP portfolios. Watch secondaries, not just tickers.

Social license is a new constraint the fiber boom never faced

Nobody held a town hall to stop fiber conduit. Datacenters drink water, bid up power, and hum next to neighborhoods — so resistance acts like a second infrastructure constraint, but with the opposite multiple effect: scarcity from physics earns a premium, scarcity from politics earns a regulatory discount and stranded-asset risk. Lab backlash compounds it, and it doesn't stay contained — in the model it leaks into open-source adoption (users hedging away from labs) and into the local-inference shift. The dashed red couplings in the diagram above are the point: sentiment is upstream of architecture.

Distributed inference routes around the bottleneck — and around the index

Our companion AI infrastructure simulator maps seven deployment architectures from gigawatt campuses to behind-the-meter micro-DCs and consumer devices, and its base case has open source carrying 40–50% of inference by 2028. The pricing implication cuts both ways: a shift to local and edge inference relieves the datacenter constraint (more usage growth gets through), but that compute happens on hardware the listed AI complex doesn't monetize. Usage can boom while hyperscaler and lab revenue capture shrinks — which is why the distributed-shift slider raises the growth ceiling while lowering the multiple. It's also where seed-stage exposure to the buildout (behind-the-meter, powered land, brownfield) diverges most sharply from holding the public complex.

Security surprises are coming — the open question is who they favor

Agentic systems with tool access, jailbreaks at scale, model-enabled attacks: expect more incidents, and expect restrictions to follow each one. The model treats security fear as a risk premium on the whole complex plus a deployment drag (security reviews, agent rollbacks, procurement freezes) — and it makes markets jumpier, so panic triggers at shallower drawdowns. The contested call is the open-source coupling, and we've made it deliberately two-sided: restrictions bind hardest on open-weight distribution (you can't recall a torrent, so regulators reach for the lever they can pull — favoring compliance-moated labs), while the same incidents make enterprises distrust sending data out, pulling inference on-prem and local — where self-hosted open models live. As modeled, fear trims open-source share modestly but accelerates the distributed shift more. If you believe the sovereignty channel dominates the restriction channel, the net flips to open source's favor — that's a parameter disagreement worth having, not a settled fact.

Labor data is the proof — and the political fuse

The best evidence that AI productivity is real won't come from earnings calls; it will show up in payroll data. Stanford's AI Economic Indicators (with ADP, tracking 730+ occupations) currently show exactly the in-between state the default slider encodes: aggregate employment divergence is modest, but early-career workers in exposed occupations are contracting nearly 4% a year while their less-exposed peers grow — and it's automation-skewed usage, not augmentation, that correlates with the declines. Their takeoff indicators stay undramatic too: TFP shows no break, capital share keeps climbing. So the model treats displacement as double-edged: it lifts effective productivity realization (the green coupling — firms don't cut headcount for a demo), but it feeds backlash and direct policy risk (AI taxes, severance mandates, deployment rules). Critically, the political channel is nonlinear: canaries don't move legislatures; aggregate numbers do. Past the midpoint of the slider the backlash coupling roughly triples — which is why the "Jobs shock" preset produces the model's purest divergence: revenue grows hard on proven productivity while the multiple gets repriced by the policy response. The strongest fundamental scenario and the strongest political reaction are the same scenario.

This time capex is funded by cash flow, not debt

The hyperscalers are spending earnings, not junk bonds — so a demand disappointment transmits to equity multiples first rather than credit markets, arguing for a 2022-style 50–60% growth-stock drawdown over a 2008-style systemic event. Unless debt-funded neocloud buildouts change that mix. Watch the financing channel, because it determines which historical overlay applies.
